# AI in Education Digest — 26 June 2026

---

**[International / Higher Education Research]** — Environmental and climate researchers are increasingly turning to AI tools—large language models for literature synthesis, machine learning for dataset analysis, and generative models for coding and writing—even as they publish on the very ecological harms those tools produce, including AI's substantial water and carbon footprint. Times Higher Education examines the structural contradiction within environmental academia, where deep expertise in AI's environmental cost has yet to translate into professional norms or institutional limits on its use.

*For educators:* If your institution studies environmental harm, should it set binding targets for its own AI energy and water use—and what body would be legitimate to enforce them?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/environmental-researchers-know-impact-ai-so-why-do-they-still-use-it

---

**[United States / K–12]** — An EdSurge podcast featuring Aleta Margolis, founder of the Center for Inspired Teaching, makes the case that meaningful AI governance in schools must start with listening to students, not drafting top-down rules—because in most classrooms today, students demonstrably know more about AI than the adults writing policy around it. A RAND American Youth Panel survey cited in the episode found that only about one in three students say their school has a school-wide AI policy, meaning real practice is running well ahead of governance virtually everywhere.

*For educators:* If students already understand AI's capabilities better than many of the adults crafting school policy, what would genuine co-creation of AI guidelines with students actually look like at your institution?

Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/your-kids-know-more-about-ai-than-you-do

---

**[Thailand / Asia-Pacific]** — A survey of more than 433 higher-education institutions across Asia Pacific, shared at the 2026 Times Higher Education Asia Universities Summit, reveals wide variation in AI and digital readiness across teaching, research, connectivity, and leadership. Thailand's Chulalongkorn University was highlighted as a model for building outcome-based AI learning frameworks directly with industry partners, while Huawei's vice-president for education argued that universities—not technology companies—are the place where the human judgment to use AI wisely must be cultivated.

*For educators:* As universities across the Global South build AI capacity through industry partnerships, what aspects of academic life—curriculum design, student assessment, data governance—should institutions require partners to stay entirely out of?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/reimagining-higher-education-aidriven-future

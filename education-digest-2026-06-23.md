# AI in Education Digest — June 23, 2026

**[Australia / Global Higher Education]** — New research by an academic at Central Queensland University tested commercial AI detection tools against student scripts that were only *partially* AI-written, and found the tools consistently misidentified the proportion of AI-generated content, making them unreliable indicators of misconduct. The researcher recommends treating a detection score above 60% as just one factor alongside institutional policy and student transparency, and calls on universities to redesign assessments that don't depend on detectors at all.

*For educators:* If AI detectors can't accurately measure partial AI use — the most common real-world scenario — what would a well-designed assessment task look like that makes students' own thinking visible without relying on a score?

**[UK / Higher Education]** — A Higher Education Policy Institute study of 96 UK universities found that 41% have no publicly accessible AI policy at all, and those that do tend to "use the language of education while operating as compliance instruments" — promising critical thinking but delivering audit trails instead. Only four institutions — Durham, Stirling, Canterbury Christ Church, and Arts University Plymouth — were identified as having policies that genuinely support students rather than monitor them.

*For educators:* When you read your institution's AI policy, does it tell you what students should be *able to do* with AI, or mainly what they must *not* do — and what would flipping that emphasis change in practice?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/university-ai-policies-promise-support-deliver-surveillance

**[US / K–12]** — A Gallup and Walton Family Foundation survey of 2,069 US public school teachers found that only 18% have received formal guidance from administrators on how to use AI in their work, with 34% getting no guidance whatsoever, even though three-quarters of school districts now claim to have AI guidelines in place. The gap between policy documents at the district level and actual teacher support in classrooms points to a structural failure in how AI integration is being implemented across American schools.

*For educators:* What's the difference between a district having an AI policy and teachers actually feeling equipped to act on it — and whose job is it to close that gap?

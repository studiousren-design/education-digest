# AI in Education Digest — 2026-06-08

---

**[UK / University of Surrey]** — The University of Surrey has undertaken what it claims is the first sector-wide systematic redesign of every degree programme to embed discipline-specific AI, taking effect from September 2026. Rather than a generic AI-literacy overlay, each faculty tailored AI tools to its own disciplinary standards—engineering students learn AI-assisted modelling, law students learn AI-assisted legal research—requiring a major redraft of curricula and assessments across the institution.

*For educators:* If AI competence must now be as discipline-specific as laboratory technique or legal reasoning, should it be taught by subject faculty rather than a central digital-skills team—and what happens to departments whose staff haven't yet developed those competencies themselves?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/surrey-redesigns-all-degrees-embed-discipline-specific-ai

---

**[Europe / EU Universities]** — The EU Artificial Intelligence Act classifies AI used for student assessment as high-risk—on a par with AI deployed in hiring and credit-scoring decisions—and its transparency provisions come into force in August 2026. European universities using tools such as ChatGPT to mark student work face legally mandated requirements for human oversight, bias auditing, and algorithmic transparency, or must stop using those tools entirely, according to the European University Association.

*For educators:* If an AI-generated grade now carries the same regulatory weight as an automated hiring decision, who in your institution is legally and pedagogically responsible for signing off on each student's final mark?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/looming-eu-ai-act-could-force-universities-change-everything

---

**[Global / Research Funding]** — A study of 12 major research funders worldwide found that application volumes rose 57% on average between 2022 and 2025, with EU Marie Skłodowska-Curie fellowship applications surging 142% over three years; paradoxically, overall submission quality improved, with only 5% of applications now falling below threshold compared with 20% in 2018. Researchers warn the system risks collapse because the same AI tools used to write applications are likely being used by overwhelmed peer reviewers to assess them, hollowing out the independent human judgement that grant review exists to provide.

*For educators:* When AI can simultaneously inflate both the volume and surface polish of research proposals, what evidence of genuine scholarly originality should funders look for that AI cannot yet replicate?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/research-funders-flooded-ai-assisted-applications

**UK / European Higher Education** — A survey by Dina Kamel at the University of Portsmouth reveals a stark generational split: 41% of UK and European academics have never used generative AI in any form, and 29% have actively blocked access to AI tools in their virtual learning environments. Meanwhile, 62% of students reported feeling that their lecturers regarded them with automatic suspicion over AI use, and 57% said they hide their AI activity even when they believe it is legitimate.

*For educators:* If over half your students are concealing AI use they consider legitimate, what does that signal about the trust culture in your classroom — and whose responsibility is it to repair it?

**Australia / Higher Education** — A major Australian university is rolling out free access to frontier AI tools for all 40,000 students and staff, beginning with 5,000 licences in 2026 and completing the rollout by 2027, on the grounds that AI literacy is impossible without equitable AI access. The initiative responds to findings that nearly 80% of Australian students already use generative AI in some form, but many arrive with vastly uneven preparation depending on their socioeconomic background.

*For educators:* If your institution made advanced AI tools universally free tomorrow, what curricular or assessment changes would need to happen first to ensure access translates into genuine learning rather than dependency?

**European Union / Higher Education** — A policy expert warns that even routine, informal AI use by academics — such as deploying tools to assist with grading student work — could trigger compliance obligations under the EU AI Act's high-risk classification rules, leaving universities facing substantial legal and operational overhaul. With the Act's August 2026 high-risk enforcement deadline approaching, institutions across France, Germany, Spain and the wider bloc may need to audit and restructure how AI is embedded in teaching and administration.

*For educators:* Which everyday AI tasks in your institution might unknowingly qualify as "high-risk" under new legislation — and do you have a process for assessing whether the tools you use carry legal liability?

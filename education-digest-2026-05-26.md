# Education + AI Digest — 26 May 2026

---

**[UK / Higher Education]** — A study published in *Assessment & Evaluation in Higher Education* warns that AI tools designed to generate written student feedback are threatening "20 years' worth of progress" in marking practices; researchers argue that automating comment-writing reduces feedback from an active pedagogical *process* to an interchangeable *product*. Educators who routinely outsource this work to AI risk gradually losing the professional expertise needed to design effective assessments and respond meaningfully to student thinking.
*For educators:* If it is the act of writing feedback—not just the comment itself—that sharpens your understanding of what students know, what do you lose when AI writes it for you?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

**[Singapore / University]** — Researchers at Nanyang Technological University have published a four-mode pedagogical framework asking students to engage with AI along a continuum: learning *from* its outputs, using it *with* as a collaborative thinking partner, studying what AI *is about* through critical inquiry, and finally producing work that goes *beyond* what AI can generate alone. The model holds that teachers must work through all four modes themselves before they can guide students effectively, repositioning the educator as a co-learner in navigating AI rather than a gatekeeper of it.
*For educators:* What would it mean to redesign a major assessment so that the final product could only have been created by human collective reasoning—and how would you know?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

---

**[UK / Higher Education Workforce]** — A Times Higher Education survey of UK university staff finds 55% do not fear redundancy due to AI within three years, yet experts caution that the more likely threat is institutions using AI efficiency arguments as cover for broader restructuring—merging roles, automating administrative work, and redistributing labour onto fewer people. The disconnect between staff confidence and expert assessment points to a sector that may be poorly prepared to distinguish genuine productivity gains from AI-justified cost-cutting.
*For educators:* As AI absorbs tasks at the edges of your role, how would you identify the moment when "efficiency" has become a reason your institution needs fewer of you?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

**[UK / Higher Education]** — The University of Surrey has undertaken what it claims is a sector-first systematic redesign of every one of its degree programmes to embed discipline-specific AI from September 2026, explicitly rejecting the add-on "AI literacy module" approach. The redesign reaches across faculties: politics students must interrogate ChatGPT's electoral analysis against the British Election Study dataset and established voting-behaviour theory; civil engineering students use AI to design a six-storey hotel but must verify every output with hand calculations and industry-standard modelling.

*For educators:* When AI is taught as a tool accountable to disciplinary standards rather than as a generic productivity aid, does that fundamentally change the critical relationship students develop with both the subject and the model's limitations?

**[EU & Global / Research Funding]** — A study by the Research on Research Institute found grant-application volumes surged 57% between 2022 and 2025 across major funders, with EU Marie Skłodowska-Curie early-career fellowship applications rising 142% in three years — a spike attributed largely to AI-assisted writing flattening the quality floor. Reviewers now face volumes of polished submissions so large that selection risks becoming arbitrary; the US National Institutes of Health responded by banning AI-written applications outright in July 2025, while European funders are still formulating responses.

*For educators:* If AI equalises grant-writing quality across institutions and career stages, what new criteria — beyond the polished document itself — should funding bodies develop to genuinely surface research merit and potential?

**[UK / Education Research]** — A teacher-researcher with a University of Exeter PhD spent nine months documenting suspected AI-generated phrases in over 100 papers published in British Educational Research Association journals, finding that phrases such as "underscoring the intricate interplay between" appeared 3,004 times in post-2023 publications compared with just 26 times before. BERA launched a formal investigation but ultimately cleared most flagged papers, prompting warnings from the researcher that continued tolerance of AI-padded academic output will erode the trust that practitioners place in university-generated evidence.

*For educators:* When AI-generated language floods the peer-reviewed research that teachers rely on to make evidence-based classroom decisions, how can practitioners develop reliable ways to distinguish genuine insight from authoritative-sounding filler?

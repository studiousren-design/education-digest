**[Australia / Higher Education]** — Researchers at Deakin University warn that wearable AI devices — smart glasses, earbuds, rings, pendants, watches, and hearing aids — represent a "second wave" of AI disruption to academic assessment that institutions have no reliable means of detecting or policing, noting that "a rule that cannot be enforced does not preserve evidential confidence." Rather than blanket bans, they recommend structural redesigns: supervised essays, randomised oral-examination questions, or mandatory tutor sign-off on lab work that builds validity into assessment design itself.

*For educators:* If wearable AI makes technological surveillance of exams unenforceable, which assessment formats do you trust most to measure genuine understanding — and why?

**[Europe / Higher Education]** — A senior European University Association official warns that the EU AI Act — as it comes into full force for high-risk AI systems — could compel universities across EU member states to fundamentally overhaul their AI practices, with automated grading and AI-driven student assessment singled out as applications likely to trigger strict documentation, mandatory human oversight, and transparency obligations. The regulation's reach covers any AI tool classified as "high risk," a category that already encompasses many adaptive-learning and automated-assessment platforms embedded in university workflows across non-English-speaking Europe.

*For educators:* How would you redesign your institution's AI-powered assessment tools if regulators required you to document — and justify — every decision the system makes about a student?

**[UK / Higher Education]** — The London School of Innovation has become the first institution in the UK — and reportedly the world — to receive regulatory approval from the Office for Students to award postgraduate degrees through AI-led teaching, with one-year online master's programmes in machine learning, digital innovation, entrepreneurship, and business transformation opening to students in June 2026. Each student is assigned an AI "private tutor" that adapts content to their individual background, pace, and learning preferences, delivers continuous formative assessment via quizzes and simulations, and conducts a "Socratic dialogue" at the close of every module — with human module leaders and a student-success team in a support role, and lectures replaced entirely by the AI.

*For educators:* When an AI tutor can personalise, assess, and reflect with a student throughout an entire master's degree, what irreplaceable role should a human educator still play?

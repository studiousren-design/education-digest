# Education Digest — 2026-05-14

**[Global — Higher Education]** — Researchers and educators are warning that students' habitual outsourcing of thinking to AI chatbots is accumulating a "cognitive debt", eroding the deep-reading, debate, and analytical-writing practices that university education is designed to build. A new commentary in THE Campus argues that deliberately assigning debate-style writing tasks — where students must commit pen-to-paper positions and defend them — is one of the most direct ways to repay that debt and rebuild the judgement and rhetorical awareness AI use is quietly degrading.
*For educators:* If AI use has already depleted students' cognitive reserves before they reach your class, whose responsibility is remediation — the individual instructor, the programme, or the institution — and what would a genuine structural response look like?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/think-again-reclaiming-our-cognitive-debt-ai

**[Asia — Japan, Indonesia, Thailand, Singapore, Hong Kong]** — A new survey of universities across five Asian education systems finds that while AI professional development is widespread (Thailand leading at 86% participation), deep infrastructure gaps remain: only 44% of institutions have 5G access, and virtual-tutor adoption stands at just 12% in Japan and 14% in Indonesia compared with 64% in Singapore. Formal recognition of AI competence in staff recruitment and promotion is rare outside Singapore (73%), meaning that even where faculty build AI skills, institutional incentives to sustain them are largely absent.
*For educators:* When a lecturer's AI proficiency depends on which country — or which city — their university sits in, how should international curricula and research collaborations account for such stark within-region inequality?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/more-coordinated-investment-needed-unlock-asian-ai-potential

**[International — Research Universities]** — A study of 12 research funders worldwide by the Research on Research Institute finds that grant applications have surged an average of 57% since ChatGPT's 2022 launch, with applications to the EU's Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions early-career fellowships rising 142% over three years. In response, the US National Institutes of Health barred AI-generated applications from July 2025, while other funders are debating lotteries, tighter screening, and new reviewer-support systems to cope with the AI-driven flood.
*For educators:* If AI tools allow any researcher to produce a polished-looking application, does high volume make peer review less reliable as a signal of quality — and what might replace it?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/research-funders-flooded-ai-assisted-applications

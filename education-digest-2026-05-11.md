# AI Education Digest — 11 May 2026

---

**[UK higher education]** — A UK university has launched a formal pilot of AI-assisted marking and feedback, framing its primary goal as reducing staff workload rather than displacing human assessors, with officials stressing that using AI to "cut humans out the loop" is explicitly not the intention. Early student feedback from the trial consistently affirms that students still value assessment from academics, even as the institution tests whether AI can handle initial review at scale.

*For educators:* If students say they want human feedback even when AI marking is available, where does the ethical line fall between institutional efficiency gains and the educational experience students are paying for?

**[Global / US higher education]** — A US startup called Companion launched an AI chatbot marketed as "Einstein AI" that explicitly offered to watch recorded lectures on a student's behalf, participate in online discussions and submit assignments directly from their accounts — moving well beyond study aid into explicit contract cheating-as-a-service. After a wave of online backlash, the tool was pulled not because of any academic integrity enforcement but because CMG Worldwide, acting for the Hebrew University of Jerusalem (the sole owner of Einstein's intellectual property), issued a trademark cease-and-desist.

*For educators:* When a contract-cheating service is dismantled by trademark law before any university disciplinary mechanism acts, what does that reveal about the speed and adequacy of higher education's response to AI-enabled academic dishonesty?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

---

**[Canada — University of Waterloo]** — Vivek Goel, stepping down as president and vice-chancellor of the University of Waterloo in June, warned that institutions relying solely on lectures and essays "will not survive for very long" given AI disruption and growing scepticism over high tuition fees. He highlighted Waterloo's co-operative education programme — which alternates students between four-month academic terms and four-month full-time work placements — as a structural model genuinely difficult for AI or rival institutions to replicate, calling it the institution's "secret sauce."

*For educators:* If work-integrated learning is increasingly framed as a structural hedge against AI disruption, does that recast universities primarily as experiential and credentialing brokers rather than as knowledge institutions?

# Education Digest — 18 May 2026

---

**[UK — British Educational Research Association]** — A leading UK education research publisher is under pressure to retract dozens more papers after an initial wave of 19 retractions triggered by peer-review failures linked to AI-generated "slop" — content critics say was clearly machine-produced and should never have cleared editorial review. A prominent commentator argues these retractions must mark the start of a systematic, sector-wide clean-up rather than an isolated incident, warning that teacher trust in education research is at stake.
*For educators:* When AI-generated text can pass peer review in the very journals that underpin evidence-based teaching, how much confidence can practitioners place in recent education research — and what would a responsible audit of the literature you rely on actually look like?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/retractions-must-be-start-ai-slop-clean-says-critic

**[UK — University of Hull, Healthcare Professional Education]** — A University of Hull educator argues that health curricula are dangerously out of sync with clinical reality: AI tools are already embedded in diagnosis and patient management, yet faculty debate centres on whether students should be allowed to use AI to write essays. The piece calls for structured AI literacy — covering safety, critical evaluation, and professional accountability — to be woven directly into healthcare programmes, with the explicit goal of preparing graduates to question, audit, or override an AI recommendation in a clinical setting.
*For educators:* If the students you graduate this year will routinely work alongside AI diagnostic tools within five years, does your curriculum teach them how to challenge the machine — or only how to use it?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/what-does-ai-literacy-healthcare-look

---

**[Global — Higher Education Research Culture]** — A Times Higher Education analysis warns that AI-generated summaries — served automatically by Google, ChatGPT, and similar platforms — are quietly displacing the messy, intellectually formative stage of research: the open-ended search, source comparison, and grappling with contradictory evidence that higher education is designed to cultivate. Because these summaries can elevate marginal or atypical viewpoints as authoritative and representative, students may arrive at their first lecture with a pre-formed, algorithmically filtered picture of a field before they have read a single primary source.
*For educators:* If a student's mental model of your discipline was largely assembled by AI summaries before they walked into your classroom, what kind of "prior knowledge" are they actually bringing — and how would you even know?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/how-ai-quietly-distorting-academic-enquiry-and-what-do-about-it

**[US Higher Education]** — A Berkeley (CSHE) working paper analysing more than 500,000 grades from 2018–2025 at a large Texas research university found that courses most exposed to generative AI saw the share of A grades rise by 13 percentage points — roughly 30 per cent relative to the pre-ChatGPT baseline. The gains were concentrated in homework-heavy writing and coding assignments, leading researcher Igor Chirikov to conclude students are using AI to inflate grades rather than deepen learning.
*For educators:* If AI is boosting grades specifically on unsupervised take-home work, should high-stakes assessment shift entirely to observed, in-person settings — and what would that trade-off cost in authentic, project-based learning?

**[East & Southeast Asia]** — Data from the Times Higher Education Digital Maturity Index reveals a stark divide in AI adoption: 64 per cent of Singaporean and 46 per cent of Hong Kong universities have deployed virtual-tutor systems, compared with only 14 per cent in Japan and 12 per cent in Indonesia. Researchers argue that fragmented national investment strategies are holding back the region's AI potential despite generally strong digital infrastructure.
*For educators:* What institutional or policy conditions explain why some Asian university systems have adopted AI tutoring at scale while neighbouring systems lag so far behind — and should adoption pace itself be treated as a meaningful goal?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/more-coordinated-investment-needed-unlock-asian-ai-potential

**[UK Higher Education]** — A trial supported by sector body Jisc found that retaining genuine human oversight of AI-assisted marking is "more complex than one might initially think" and may not save academics any time. Reviewers risk being unduly influenced by the AI's feedback rather than exercising independent judgement — reducing oversight to little more than "scanning one's eyes over the student's work before clicking 'next'."
*For educators:* When human review of an AI marking tool risks becoming perfunctory box-ticking, at what point does an institution have a duty to withdraw the tool rather than simply retrain reviewers to be more vigilant?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/retaining-human-oversight-ai-marking-complex-trial-finds

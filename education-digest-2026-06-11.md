# Education Digest — 11 June 2026

---

**[UK — Education Research Integrity]** A UK teacher and doctoral researcher, Stephen Vainker, has documented suspected AI-generated writing in more than 100 papers published in British Educational Research Association (BERA) journals, tracking telltale phrases such as "underscoring the intricate interplay between," which appeared 26 times before 2023 and 3,004 times since. BERA opened an investigation in response but concluded no further action was required for the majority of flagged papers, a decision critics say risks eroding teachers' trust in academic evidence.

*For educators:* If the research base that informs classroom practice is quietly contaminated by AI-generated text that goes undeclared and unpunished, how should teachers calibrate their confidence in the studies they rely on to make pedagogical decisions?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/tackle-ai-slop-education-research-or-lose-teacher-trust

**[Scotland / UK — Higher Education Governance]** More than 350 staff at the University of Edinburgh have signed an open letter demanding the institution end its partnership with OpenAI, calling it "unsafe" and "insecure." Their concerns centre on OpenAI's history of data breaches, alleged exploitation of low-paid content moderators, and the company's February 2026 agreement to deploy its models in US military classified networks — which signatories argue is incompatible with the university's own AI and procurement principles.

*For educators:* When a university's chosen AI vendor takes actions that a significant portion of staff find ethically incompatible with institutional values, what formal governance structures should exist to give staff a meaningful say in renewing or ending that contract?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/edinburgh-staff-urge-university-ditch-openai-deal

---

**[USA — K–12 Schools]** New data from the Center for Democracy and Technology shows that approximately 85 percent of teachers and students now use AI for schoolwork, yet only one in three students reports that their school has a consistent, institution-wide policy on AI use — with most students saying rules vary by teacher. A RAND study cited alongside the data found that 67 percent of students believe heavier AI use will harm their critical thinking, suggesting students themselves are uncertain about the technology even as they adopt it.

*For educators:* Given that students are already navigating AI without clear guidance, how can individual teachers lead structured, honest classroom conversations about AI use that set shared norms — without waiting for district-level policy that may never arrive?

Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/2026-06-03-what-to-do-about-ai-begin-by-talking-about-it

# AI in Education Digest — 2026-05-22

---

**United States — Higher Education (public research university, Texas)** — A UC Berkeley researcher analysed more than 500,000 graded assignments at a large Texas public university and found that A grades in "AI-exposed" courses—those relying on writing and coding—rose by 13 percentage points (a 30% relative increase) after ChatGPT's release in late 2022. The gains were sharpest in courses where homework carried the most weight toward final grades, suggesting students are using AI to complete take-home work rather than to deepen understanding.
*For educators:* If AI-assisted homework reliably inflates grades while in-class performance stays flat, does that mean we have been measuring completion more than learning all along—and what would genuinely AI-resistant assessment look like?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/grade-inflation-much-higher-ai-exposed-degrees-says-us-study

---

**Europe — Doctoral education (217 universities, EUA-CDE survey)** — A survey by the European University Association's Council for Doctoral Education found that more than two-thirds of European institutions—spanning countries from Germany and Finland to Romania and Portugal—report their PhD students are already using AI tools in their research. Unlike the cautious tone common in undergraduate policy discussions, doctoral education leaders across the continent reported seeing "more opportunity than challenges" in the rise of AI, even as governance frameworks for AI-assisted research remain largely undeveloped.
*For educators:* If doctoral supervisors across Europe are more optimistic about AI integration than their undergraduate counterparts, should PhD programmes be treated as the sector's natural laboratory for developing responsible AI-in-research policies—and what obligations does that place on supervisors?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/two-thirds-universities-report-ai-use-among-doctoral-students

---

**United States — Early childhood education (pre-K classrooms)** — RAND Corporation research found that roughly 1 in 3 preschool teachers (29%) now uses generative AI tools at school, making early childhood educators the lowest adopters across K–12 but an earlier entry point than most expected. Teachers report using AI to draft lesson plans and create scaffolded activities rather than for direct child interaction, operating largely without any institutional policy guidance suited to the developmental sensitivities of 3- to 5-year-olds.
*For educators:* Now that generative AI has entered the pre-K classroom as a teacher-facing tool—without guardrails designed for the youngest learners—who should be setting the ethical and developmental standards that govern its use, and on what evidence should those standards rest?
Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/2026-01-05-1-in-3-pre-k-teachers-uses-generative-ai-at-school

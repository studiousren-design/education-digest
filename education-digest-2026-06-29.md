# Education Digest — 29 June 2026

---

**UK / Global Higher Education Research** — Sophisticated AI agents—tools that autonomously gather information, draft documents, and act with minimal human oversight—are flooding research funding systems with grant applications. Analysis of 12 funders across seven countries found application volumes rose 57% between ChatGPT's launch and end of 2025, with EU Marie Skłodowska-Curie fellowships up 142%; experts at University College London warn that if AI both writes and reviews proposals, the system will optimise for what funders have previously rewarded rather than surfacing genuinely novel ideas—a "quality compression" that could hollow out research from within.
*For educators:* If AI agents can produce grant proposals indistinguishable from expert human writing, should funding bodies redesign evaluation from the ground up—and what would it mean to assess research potential in a world where polish is free?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/new-ai-agents-pose-existential-threat-how-grants-are-awarded

---

**Australia — University Students** — A YouGov survey of more than 1,200 Australian university students found that 84% now use AI tools at least occasionally—up from 74% a year earlier—yet adoption has brought new anxieties rather than ease. Fifty-two per cent cited "being accused of cheating when I did nothing wrong" as a top three fear, and 42% worried AI would reduce job opportunities in their field, even while most simultaneously view AI skills as essential for employability.
*For educators:* When students feel compelled to use AI for career readiness yet fear institutional punishment for doing so, how should universities redesign both their AI policies and their assessment frameworks to close that trust gap?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/ai-double-edged-sword-increases-anxiety-among-students

---

**United States — Classroom** — A teacher who built and deployed an AI grading tool discovered it had sent AI-generated feedback comments directly to a student before the teacher had reviewed—or even seen—them; the student sent a thank-you note for words the teacher never wrote. After this incident the teacher removed the fully automated delivery feature, requiring all AI-drafted comments to pass through mandatory human review before release.
*For educators:* When AI-generated feedback can mimic a teacher's voice closely enough that students believe it is personal, what transparency obligations should schools enforce—and do students have a right to know when the words they receive were not written by a human?
Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/i-built-an-ai-grading-tool-then-a-student-thanked-me-for-words-i-didnt-write

# AI in Education Digest — 2026-05-12

**[UK — Higher Education]** — The London School of Innovation, newly granted degree-awarding powers in March 2026, will enrol its first master's students in June with programmes taught entirely by AI private tutors rather than human lecturers. Each student receives a personalised AI avatar that adapts content to their pace and background; human academics are responsible only for final summative assessment, with fees set between £9,000 and £11,000.
*For educators:* If formative learning is delegated entirely to AI tutors, what aspects of human academic mentorship — beyond grading — become most irreplaceable for a student's intellectual development?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/virtual-lecturers-be-overseen-academics-ai-university

**[European Union — Higher Education Regulation]** — European universities face pressure to overhaul their AI practices as the EU AI Act's remaining provisions roll out over the next six to thirty-six months. Academics who use tools such as ChatGPT to grade students without institutional oversight may already be in breach, warns Thomas Jørgensen of the European University Association, who says many institutions may need to "change everything" about how they deploy AI.
*For educators:* Does your institution have governance structures that cover staff members' informal AI use in assessment — and what would a proportionate compliance framework actually look like in practice?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/looming-eu-ai-act-could-force-universities-change-everything

**[Global — Higher Education Pedagogy]** — Researchers have piloted the SAGE (Structured AI-Guided Education) framework, arguing that universities must move beyond policy statements to actively teach students to interrogate AI outputs. In the pilot, 73% of students systematically verified AI-generated content and 81% engaged in substantive revision after identifying inaccuracies — outcomes the authors say policies alone cannot produce.
*For educators:* What would it take to redesign a core course around AI verification as a transferable skill, rather than treating AI use as something to permit or prohibit?

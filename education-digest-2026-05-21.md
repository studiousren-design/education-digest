# AI in Education Digest — 21 May 2026

---

**[Global / Academic Research Publishing]** — arXiv, the world's largest preprint server, announced it will impose an immediate one-year submission ban on any author found to have knowingly submitted content containing AI-generated hallucinations — such as fabricated citations or LLM meta-comments left in the manuscript text — with "incontrovertible evidence" set as the trigger standard. Research integrity campaigners have welcomed the policy as a meaningful deterrent, but critics warn that reliably distinguishing AI-generated prose from human writing remains technically inconsistent, raising concerns about uneven enforcement across disciplines.
*For educators:* If even a well-resourced global repository acknowledges the difficulty of detecting AI-generated content reliably, how should institutions calibrate their own AI-detection policies to avoid penalising false positives while still upholding academic standards?

**[UK / Higher Education Degree Programmes]** — The London School of Innovation has become the first UK institution to receive formal degree-awarding powers specifically validated for a fully AI-led teaching model: each postgraduate student is assigned a personalised AI tutor, navigates module content through AI avatars, and engages in Socratic dialogue sessions with the AI — with the option to request a one-to-one session with a human module leader at any time. Human academics act as supervisory overseers rather than primary instructors, and the institution has launched an "academic innovation cluster" to research the effectiveness of AI-delivered learning ahead of its first cohort arriving in June 2026.
*For educators:* Now that a national regulator has formally certified an institution where AI leads instruction and humans play a supporting role, what specific outcomes — cognitive, social, professional — should traditional universities be able to demonstrate that human-led teaching uniquely produces?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/virtual-lecturers-be-overseen-academics-ai-university

**[UK / Multi-Disciplinary Higher Education]** — A Times Higher Education Campus analysis argues that blanket, institution-wide AI training programmes systematically fail students because the meaning of AI competence varies fundamentally by field: an engineering student described AI as "augmentation, not replacement" for repetitive tasks while retaining responsibility for core logic; a graphic design student drew a firm line against AI as author; and professional programmes in law, medicine, and nursing require accountability frameworks explicitly tied to regulatory obligations. The piece contends that only guidance built at the module level — specific to the discipline, the assessment type, and the stage of study — can develop genuine AI literacy rather than surface familiarity with tools.
*For educators:* If AI literacy is genuinely discipline-specific rather than a transferable generic skill, who at your institution currently holds the authority and expertise to define what it means within your field — and what mechanisms ensure that definition is consistently taught across cohorts?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/why-ai-literacy-must-be-discipline-specific

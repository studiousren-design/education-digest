# Education AI Digest — 2026-05-29

---

**[Global Academic Publishing]** — arXiv, the preprint repository used by researchers across every discipline, has announced it will impose an immediate one-year ban on authors found to have submitted papers containing AI-generated text with hallucinated references or other demonstrably AI-produced inaccuracies. Research integrity experts welcome the principle but warn it cannot realistically be enforced at scale: thousands of manuscripts containing AI-generated problems are estimated to reach the platform every year, and each case requires staff adjudication and an appeals process.

*For educators:* If the academic publishing system cannot reliably police AI content standards, what verification responsibility should teachers place on students — and themselves — before assigning or citing preprint research?

**[Hong Kong — Education University of Hong Kong]** — Bruce Macfarlane, dean at the Education University of Hong Kong, argues that the widespread "moral panic" over student AI use is causing universities to ignore endemic "micro-cheating" by academics themselves — practices such as publishing near-identical papers twice ("double-dipping") and inflating impact through excessive self-citation. He contends this institutionalised hypocrisy is statistically far more common than the dramatic retractions that make headlines, and that calling students out for surface-learning while tolerating the same behaviour in faculty corrodes the entire system's credibility.

*For educators:* When you enforce AI integrity rules for students, do the researchers behind your reading lists hold themselves to equivalent standards — and what would it look like to make that question part of the curriculum?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/endemic-micro-cheating-academics-going-unpunished

---

**[United Kingdom — London School of Innovation]** — The London School of Innovation has become the first institution in the UK to receive degree-awarding powers for programmes delivered primarily through AI virtual tutors, after the Office for Students completed its approval process. Each enrolled student is assigned an AI private tutor that adapts content to their individual pace, background, and learning style, with human lecturers available on request but not required; the inaugural master's cohort is scheduled to begin in June 2026.

*For educators:* Now that an AI-taught degree carries the same official credential as a traditional one, what evidence of genuine understanding — beyond successful task completion — should institutions and employers demand from graduates?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/virtual-lecturers-be-overseen-academics-ai-university

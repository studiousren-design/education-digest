# Education + AI Digest — 27 May 2026

---

**[USA / Tulane University]** — Award-winning biographer and Tulane University history professor Walter Isaacson has made AI use mandatory in all his history courses, requiring students to work with two or three different AI models and document how they used AI at every stage of their research. Isaacson argues that blanket bans are not only futile but a wasted opportunity, framing AI tools as a "wonderful arrow in the quiver of the historian" — a stance that directly contradicts colleagues who prohibit AI in history curricula entirely.
*For educators:* If a practitioner-historian of Isaacson's standing sees AI as an integral research tool rather than a threat to rigour, what would it mean to redesign a history assignment so that AI use was required, transparent, and itself a subject of critical reflection — rather than a prohibited shortcut?

**[International / Research Funding Bodies]** — A survey of 12 research funders worldwide by the Research on Research Institute found that grant application volumes rose by an average of 57% between 2022 and 2025 — the years since ChatGPT launched — with applications to the EU's Marie Skłodowska-Curie early-career fellowships surging 142% in three years. In response, the US National Institutes of Health has ruled AI-drafted applications ineligible for funding, while other agencies warn that reviewers could soon face volumes so large that allocation of prestigious grants becomes effectively arbitrary.
*For educators:* If the sheer volume of AI-assisted applications begins to swamp peer review, how should universities help doctoral students and early-career researchers demonstrate genuine scholarly depth in a landscape where a polished proposal no longer signals meaningful effort?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

---

**[India / International Student Recruitment]** — Companies including Edysor — an Indian education consultancy deploying AI voices in multiple languages to handle thousands of simultaneous student enquiries — and INSELECT, which markets itself as offering "no humans, no hassle" automated admissions for universities in the UK, US, and Australia, are rapidly expanding AI into the overseas student recruitment pipeline. INTO University Partnerships reports that AI has cut application processing from weeks to hours, but critics raise unresolved concerns about systems that show "sexist and racist tendencies" in candidate screening and leave prospective students with no meaningful right to opt out.
*For educators:* When AI determines which international applicants ever receive a human conversation during admissions, who bears responsibility for auditing whether the system's embedded biases are systematically narrowing the diversity of the incoming cohort?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

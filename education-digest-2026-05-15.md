# Education Digest — 2026-05-15

**[Global — University Humanities]** — An instructor designing a course around Holocaust survivor video testimonies assigns students to watch recordings from archives such as the Fortunoff Video Archive before uploading transcripts to AI tools and prompting the model to interpret trauma and meaning; AI reliably retrieves surface facts but fails catastrophically at significance — flagging a scene at Auschwitz as "a comforting reunion" when it described tragedy. The sequencing is deliberate: students encounter the testimony as humans first, so that when the AI misreads it they can articulate precisely what the machine missed, turning the model's interpretive limits into the core lesson about critical understanding.
*For educators:* Which moments in your discipline's source material contain the deepest "meaning gaps" — where an AI would get the facts right and the significance catastrophically wrong — and could revealing those gaps become the assignment rather than a warning label?

**[Hong Kong — Higher Education]** — Bruce Macfarlane, dean of the Faculty of Education at the Education University of Hong Kong, argues in a newly published paper that universities' AI-fuelled "moral panic" over student cheating is masking far more widespread micro-cheating by academics themselves: "double dipping" near-identical publications, excessive self-citation, and "symbolic citation" of sources never actually read. He contends these practices are endemic and far more common than the headline retraction cases that dominate institutional attention, and that the same AI tools intensifying scrutiny of students may be making subtle academic dishonesty easier to conceal.
*For educators:* If the transparency standards now applied to students' AI use were applied equally to your own citation and publication practices, which everyday academic shortcuts would have to be reclassified as misconduct?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/endemic-micro-cheating-academics-going-unpunished

**[International — Higher Education]** — A THE Campus analysis argues that the dominant institutional response to AI — detection and prohibition — fundamentally misframes the problem, because every workplace graduates enter will expect fluent AI use; the productive question is not "Did the student use AI?" but "How did they use it, and did those choices serve the task?" The piece sets out a framework for redesigning assessments so that AI interactions are disclosed, visible, and themselves subject to evaluation, making the quality of a student's AI collaboration a graded competency rather than a conduct issue.
*For educators:* If your next assignment required students to document and justify every AI interaction as part of their submission, what would that audit reveal about the difference between using AI to think and using AI to avoid thinking?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/assessing-students-when-artificial-intelligence-ubiquitous

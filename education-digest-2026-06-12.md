# Education & AI Digest — 12 June 2026

---

**[USA / K–12 Schools]** — Schools moving toward AI-first teaching models are discovering that generative AI carries unavoidable "inference costs" — per-query compute charges that accumulate with every student interaction, unlike traditional software that becomes cheaper at scale. Districts that committed early to AI platforms are now confronting structural funding gaps that strain ed-tech budgets and risk deepening inequity between well-funded and under-resourced schools.

*For educators:* If the economics of generative AI consistently favour wealthier districts that can absorb high per-student costs, what obligations do policymakers and edtech vendors have to ensure under-resourced schools are not priced out of an AI-first future?

Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/can-schools-afford-an-ai-first-future

---

**[UK / Higher Education]** — A year-long Jisc trial involving 10 UK universities testing the Graide AI feedback-and-marking platform has found that retaining genuine human oversight of AI-generated marks is "more complex than one might initially think" and may not save academics any time. Researchers identified a persistent tension between deploying AI tools efficiently and ensuring that human review is substantive rather than a rubber-stamp on automated outputs.

*For educators:* What would genuinely meaningful human oversight of AI marking look like in your course — and how would students or auditors be able to tell the difference between real review and a perfunctory sign-off?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/retaining-human-oversight-ai-marking-complex-trial-finds

---

**[UK / Higher Education Policy]** — A Higher Education Policy Institute audit of all 163 UK degree-awarding institutions found that 41% have no publicly discoverable AI policy, and of those that do, most use the "language of education while operating as compliance instruments" — promising critical-thinking support but delivering audit trails and detection mechanisms. The report concludes that universities have largely absorbed AI governance into existing misconduct frameworks rather than designing new educational responses.

*For educators:* Does your institution's AI policy read as a resource designed to help students learn with AI, or as a document primarily intended to catch and punish misuse — and does that framing shape how students actually engage with it?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/university-ai-policies-promise-support-deliver-surveillance

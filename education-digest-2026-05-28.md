# AI in Education Digest — 2026-05-28

**[European Union — Higher Education]** — European universities face a crunch-point compliance deadline in August 2026 under the EU Artificial Intelligence Act, which classifies AI used for student assessment, admissions screening, and academic progress monitoring as "high-risk." Experts warn that even informal uses — such as academics using ChatGPT to grade essays — could violate the law by failing transparency and human-oversight requirements that most popular tools don't currently meet.
*For educators:* If EU-wide compliance forces universities to document and justify every AI touchpoint in assessment, could that scrutiny actually improve the quality and fairness of feedback — or will it simply drive informal AI use underground?

**[United States — K–12 Schools]** — With school counsellor shortages at historic levels, some US districts are weighing whether to formalise AI chatbots as a "first line of defence" for student mental health, checking in regularly with students and flagging those who need professional support. Research has found that many teenagers are already voluntarily confiding personal struggles to AI tools — without any institutional framework, oversight, or safeguards in place.
*For educators:* If students are turning to AI for emotional support regardless of school policy, is deliberate institutional inaction the more dangerous option — or does formalising AI in mental health open a door that cannot be safely closed?
Source: https://www.edsurge.com/rss

**[UK / Global — Higher Education]** — Students at universities in the UK and beyond are reporting heightened anxiety over the possibility of being falsely flagged by AI-detection tools such as Turnitin, with documented cases of academic penalties imposed on the basis of suspicion rather than proof. Non-native English speakers appear disproportionately affected, with some students describing deliberate shifts to simpler prose to avoid triggering detection algorithms — a form of linguistic self-censorship with clear equity implications.
*For educators:* Given that no AI detector achieves reliable accuracy, what burden of proof and procedural protections should universities guarantee students before any academic penalty is imposed?

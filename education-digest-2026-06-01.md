# Education Digest — 1 June 2026

---

**[UK / Graduate Employment]** — The Institute of Student Employers' *Student Development Survey 2026*, drawing on 144 organisations, found that 35 per cent of employers now rate new graduates as "below expectations" on adapting to workplace demands — up from 31 per cent for school-leavers. More strikingly, 54 per cent of employers named AI's reshaping of entry-level roles as their single biggest hiring concern, yet reported that most candidates arriving for interview show little awareness of how AI is already changing day-to-day work in their sector.
*For educators:* If the adaptability gap is widening precisely because AI is automating the routine tasks that once broke graduates into professional life, should universities deliberately engineer "adaptive failure" experiences — intentionally novel, tool-free problems — into every degree programme?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/employers-fear-graduates-lack-adaptability-ai-reshapes-work

**[UK / Academic Research Integrity]** — Nineteen papers have been retracted from journals affiliated with the British Educational Research Association (BERA) after independent investigation by publisher Wiley revealed that the peer-review process for a guest-edited special section had been systematically manipulated, with the retractions following months of public documentation by teacher-researcher Stephen Vainker of AI-generated "slop" phrases appearing across more than 100 BERA-affiliated articles. The critic who first raised the alarm argues the retractions, while welcome, represent only the most visible tip of a much larger problem of AI-assisted text infiltrating education research literature.
*For educators:* When AI-generated academic papers can pass peer review in bulk, what changes to how we teach research-reading skills — and how we evaluate the trustworthiness of the literature we assign to students — are now non-negotiable?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/retractions-must-be-start-ai-slop-clean-says-critic

---

**[USA / Higher Education Value]** — The College Board's *Education Pays 2026* report shows that even as AI adoption among US employees nearly doubled from 21 per cent in 2023 to 40 per cent in 2025, college graduates continue to outperform non-graduates on employment, earnings, and long-term career resilience. Employers surveyed by Western Governors University (n = 3,000+) increasingly identify critical thinking, nuanced judgement, and the capacity for independent learning — skills cultivated by degree study — as the specific human qualities that AI cannot replicate, suggesting that the degree's labour-market value is shifting from credential to cognitive foundation.
*For educators:* If the degree's competitive advantage is now framed around what AI cannot do rather than what graduates know, how explicitly are institutions communicating — and assessing — those distinctively human capacities during students' four years on campus?
Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/2026-05-28-why-college-degrees-matter-in-the-age-of-ai

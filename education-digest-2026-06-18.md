# AI in Education Digest — 18 June 2026

**[Australia / Universities]** — A survey of more than 1,200 Australian university students found that 84% now use AI tools at least occasionally, yet widespread adoption has produced a hierarchy of new anxieties: cognitive atrophy, privacy violations, unfair grade competition, and groundless cheating accusations. Students with disabilities or special educational needs were almost four times as likely as their peers to experience "a lot of stress" when using AI tools — raising sharp questions about equity in AI-mandated learning environments.

*For educators:* When the students most likely to benefit from AI-assisted learning are also those most stressed by it, what obligations do institutions have to co-design AI policies with disabled students before rolling them out?

**[UK / University Infrastructure]** — Weeks after the criminal group ShinyHunters compromised Canvas — the AI-enhanced learning management system used by thousands of universities worldwide — the same gang seized records of hundreds of thousands of students and alumni from the University of Nottingham by exploiting an Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day. Security experts warn that similar attacks on university learning systems are a "case of when, not if," noting that 68% of the 100-plus organisations globally hit by the same exploit are in the higher-education sector.

*For educators:* As AI-enabled learning platforms centralise ever-richer student-activity data, does the pedagogical value of granular analytics justify concentrating so much sensitive information in systems that are now prime ransomware targets?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/more-nottingham-style-cyberattacks-case-when-not-if

**[US / K–12]** — A teacher overwhelmed by 450 ungraded assignments built an AI grading tool that drafts personalised comments against his rubric — but when a student emotionally thanked him for feedback he had not actually written, he removed the auto-return feature entirely. He replaced it with a mandatory human-review dashboard where every AI-drafted grade and comment requires the teacher's eyes and explicit approval before reaching a student, preserving accountability while still eliminating the backlog.

*For educators:* If AI-generated feedback creates genuine learning moments for students, does the authenticity of that feedback depend on who wrote it — or only on whether a thoughtful human stood accountable for every word before it was sent?

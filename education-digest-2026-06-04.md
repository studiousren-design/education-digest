**[US / K–12 Schools]** — The 2026 CoSN State of Ed Tech report finds that 79 per cent of school districts now have AI guidelines — up from 57 per cent in 2025 — yet only 41 per cent of AI initiatives target teaching and learning, with the majority focused on operational efficiency. Cybersecurity anxieties dominate: 98 per cent of school IT officials worry that AI will introduce new forms of cyber attack, while 82 per cent of teachers say they have never received formal guidance on using AI responsibly in their classrooms.
*For educators:* If nearly all districts are adopting AI guidelines but three-quarters of those initiatives bypass the classroom entirely, who is actually responsible for ensuring teachers can use AI safely and effectively — the district, the vendor, or the profession itself?

**[UK / Higher Education]** — A joint report by the Higher Education Policy Institute and Advance HE finds that universities are piling on assessments in response to AI: summative assignments per semester have risen from 5 (2020) to 5.8 (2025), and formative assignments from 2.5 to 4.1 — raising serious concerns about student burnout alongside the question of whether heavier workloads can actually catch AI-assisted work. Josh Freeman of HEPI warns that AI is "reducing the accuracy of assessments as a measure of students' performance," with 65 per cent of students reporting that their assessment regime has changed significantly.
*For educators:* If adding more assessments is both exhausting students and failing to deter AI use, what would a genuinely AI-resistant assessment strategy look like — and is in-person examination the only honest answer?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/universities-risk-over-assessing-response-ai

**[UK / Higher Education]** — Times Higher Education's UK University Redundancy Survey finds that 55 per cent of university workers do not fear AI will make them redundant within three years, with only 5 per cent strongly agreeing they face that risk — yet experts warn that institutions may use AI efficiency gains as quiet "justification" for restructuring regardless. Professional services staff and library workers expressed the most unease, suggesting that support functions are already being hollowed out even as academic staff remain broadly sanguine about their own futures.
*For educators:* If universities capture AI-driven savings through staff cuts rather than reinvesting them in teaching and student support, how should academic communities engage with AI governance decisions before restructuring announcements arrive?

# Education Digest — 2026-05-13

**[USA — Liberal Arts College]** Agnes Scott College in Georgia is embedding AI literacy into both required first-year courses of its four-year Summit programme beginning Fall 2026, guaranteeing that every entering student builds foundational knowledge of AI ethics and responsible use. Rather than focusing on specific tools, the curriculum centres critical thinking around bias, privacy, labour displacement, and environmental impact — a structural mandate authored by the college's president and published in THE Campus on 12 May.
*For educators:* If AI literacy becomes a graduation requirement rather than an elective, how does that change whose responsibility it is to teach it — and what gets displaced in the curriculum to make room?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/why-ai-literacy-belongs-firstyear-experience

**[USA — Higher Education Pedagogy]** A Virginia Tech music theory professor argues in THE Campus that Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles — which already require instructors to offer multiple means of engagement and representation — provide the most equitable scaffold for building AI proficiency across diverse student bodies. The piece proposes that institutions adopt UDL-centred course redesign as the systematic mechanism for closing an AI competence gap that currently depends on a student's major or instructor.
*For educators:* UDL was designed to remove barriers for students with disabilities; does redeploying it as a framework for AI literacy risk diluting its original purpose, or does the fit prove the framework's broader versatility?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/how-universal-design-learning-can-build-ai-proficiency

---

**[UK — University Workforce]** A Times Higher Education survey of UK university staff found that 55 percent do not believe AI will make them redundant within three years, with scepticism highest among STEM academics and senior managers. Experts warn the confidence is misplaced: even where AI does not directly replace roles, cash-strapped institutions may use automation as justification to cut professional-services headcount — a group already significantly more anxious than academics about the threat.
*For educators:* If university support staff bear a disproportionate share of AI-driven job losses, what does that mean for the student experience that academic staff depend on those colleagues to deliver?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/ai-risk-university-jobs-despite-staff-believing-roles-are-safe

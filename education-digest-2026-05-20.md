# AI in Education Digest — 20 May 2026

---

**[UK / Employer-Graduate Gap]** — A survey of 144 organisations by the UK's Institute of Student Employers finds that 35% of employers rate recent graduates as "below expectations" when it comes to adapting to AI-reshaped workplaces, while 54% name the impact of AI on entry-level roles as their single biggest hiring concern. Nearly half (48%) also report that graduates are using AI tools during the selection process in ways that misrepresent their true abilities, creating a growing trust gap between campuses and the labour market.
*For educators:* If employers now see AI-fluency and human adaptability as distinct skills—and graduates are failing to demonstrate the latter—how should universities redesign work-integrated learning so students practise authentic decision-making rather than AI-assisted performance?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/employers-fear-graduates-lack-adaptability-ai-reshapes-work

---

**[European Union / Regulatory Compliance]** — Experts warn that European universities may have to "change everything" about their AI practices once the EU AI Act's high-risk provisions come fully into force, because academic use of tools like ChatGPT for grading is classified in the same risk tier as AI used for hiring decisions and credit scoring. Thomas Jørgensen of the European University Association notes that many institutions have embedded AI into informal marking workflows without realising they will be required to meet strict transparency, human-oversight, and risk-assessment obligations.
*For educators:* Given that even a lecturer privately using a large language model to draft rubric feedback could soon constitute a regulated high-risk AI deployment, how should your institution begin auditing and governing the informal AI practices already woven into everyday teaching?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/looming-eu-ai-act-could-force-universities-change-everything

---

**[UK / AI Marking Pilot]** — The UK education non-profit Jisc is midway through a year-long trial in which 10 universities are testing the Graide platform: the system generates AI-produced provisional marks and feedback against supplied rubrics and model essays, which academics then review and approve before returning to students. Early student feedback from the trial shows learners value AI feedback as a supplement to—not a replacement for—human comment, and organisers are collecting data on whether the workflow meaningfully reduces marker workload.
*For educators:* If AI can reliably draft a first pass of feedback that staff merely refine and endorse, at what point does the reviewer role risk eroding the disciplinary judgment and professional expertise that careful marking is meant to build?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/ai-marking-trial-not-looking-replace-humans

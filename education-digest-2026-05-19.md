# Education Digest — 19 May 2026

---

**[India — Woxsen University]** — A strategist at Woxsen University argues that the wave of AI training workshops and certification programmes rolling out across higher education will not produce truly AI-literate universities, because competence embedded in individuals does not automatically become an institutional property. The piece calls instead for structural redesign: weaving AI capability into hiring criteria, curriculum governance, assessment frameworks, and infrastructure so that the institution itself functions differently, rather than simply upskilling individual staff.
*For educators:* If your institution's AI literacy effort stops at training individuals, what would it mean for AI competence to be woven into the rules, roles, and rhythms of your department itself — and who would need to change for that to happen?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/why-your-ai-training-programme-wont-create-ailiterate-university

**[Global — Higher Education Research Practice]** — A Times Higher Education guide details the specific risk that AI hallucinations pose to academic literature reviews: a fabricated citation sounds authoritative, passes casual scrutiny, and can circulate through subsequent scholarship, compounding the original error at scale. It offers a layered set of countermeasures — prompting AI to flag uncertainty rather than fill gaps, routing queries through database-grounded tools such as Elicit and Consensus, and visualising citation networks to expose implausible reference patterns before submission.
*For educators:* When a student submits a literature review written with AI assistance, what verification step would catch a plausible-sounding but non-existent source — and do your current assessment rubrics require that step at all?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/safeguards-against-genai-hallucination-literature-reviews

**[Hong Kong — Hong Kong Baptist University]** — Lecturers at Hong Kong Baptist University contend that "vibe coding" — directing AI tools to generate working software through plain-language descriptions rather than syntax — belongs in every university's GenAI literacy curriculum, precisely because it demands the skill educators already possess: articulating pedagogical intent with precision. By prototyping learning tools this way, faculty can bypass the steep technical barriers of traditional software development and build bespoke educational applications — interactive simulations, adaptive exercises, branching scenarios — within a single session.
*For educators:* If the main skill vibe coding demands is the ability to describe clearly what you want a tool to do for learners, what would you build for your students if technical implementation were no longer the obstacle?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/vibe-coding-belongs-your-universitys-genai-literacy-strategy

**[UK / Global Higher Education]** — Five academics writing in Times Higher Education reflect on four years of using generative AI in research, teaching, and outreach, offering unusually candid accounts of both genuine utility and subtle educational harm. ChatGPT scored 95 per cent on an undergraduate quantum physics exam and in one instance produced reasoning better than standard textbooks, while engineers and physicists report that AI assistance has "short-circuited the necessary pain" of debugging and conceptual struggle — eliminating the productive difficulty that builds deep intuition.
*For educators:* If AI already outscores most undergraduates in demanding technical subjects, what specific cognitive processes — beyond knowledge recall — should a physics or engineering course be designed to certify that a student can perform without AI?

**[UK Higher Education]** — An essay drawing on the Higher Education Policy Institute's Student Generative AI Survey 2026 warns that AI is causing a harm subtler than cheating: 95 per cent of UK undergraduates now use generative AI for their studies, 12 per cent include AI-generated text directly in submissions (up fourfold from 3 per cent in 2024), and students are beginning to lose confidence in their own writing and reasoning as AI tools continuously "correct" them. One student is quoted: "AI will always correct what I write, and I begin to feel I am not as good a writer as I thought I was."
*For educators:* When students depend on AI for constant validation and correction, are universities inadvertently teaching them to distrust their own thinking — and should assessment design deliberately create AI-free spaces to rebuild that lost intellectual confidence?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/opinion/ais-fluency-eroding-students-intellectual-confidence

**[UK Universities]** — A Higher Education Policy Institute audit of all 163 UK degree-awarding institutions found that 96 had publicly accessible AI policies; researchers then used AI to analyse them and discovered a "systematic gap between what policies say and what they do." While the documents invoke "critical thinking" and "student support," their practical mechanisms are audit trails for misconduct investigations — the author concludes that they "promise critical thinking but deliver audit trails" and "name support yet deliver surveillance."
*For educators:* If university AI policies are structurally designed to catch wrongdoers rather than develop learners, are they creating a surveillance culture that undermines the trust students need to experiment and learn openly — and what would a genuinely pedagogical AI policy look like?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/university-ai-policies-promise-support-deliver-surveillance

# AI in Education Digest — 1 July 2026

**UK** — A Jisc-led pilot now spanning 10 UK universities is testing whether the AI grading platform Graide can draft feedback and marks against a rubric before an academic confirms and returns it to students, while a further five institutions trial TeacherMatic for building lesson resources. Naomi Winstone, professor of educational psychology at the University of Surrey and lead author of the accompanying report, warns that if feedback is outsourced wholesale to AI, the pedagogical "craft of feedback" itself risks eroding even as cash-strapped universities lean on the tools to cut workload.
*For educators:* If an AI draft becomes the default first pass on every piece of feedback you give, what specific judgment calls or phrasing choices would you insist on making yourself, so that skill doesn't quietly atrophy?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/academics-risk-losing-craft-feedback-if-outsourced-ai

**UK** — Most university staff in a UK-wide survey say they don't believe their own jobs will be replaced by AI any time soon, even as sector experts caution that this confidence may be misplaced. The concern isn't that AI directly substitutes for academics' work, but that institutions could use "automation" as a convenient justification for job cuts that are really driven by broader cost-cutting and restructuring agendas.
*For educators:* If your institution announced restructuring "informed by AI efficiencies," what evidence would you want to see before accepting that framing rather than a cost-cutting decision dressed up in AI language?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/ai-risk-university-jobs-despite-staff-believing-roles-are-safe

**UK** — Staff at the University of Edinburgh, including several of the institution's own AI researchers, have publicly called on the university to end its contract with OpenAI, arguing the deal conflicts with the values and independence the university claims to uphold. The campaign, backed by members of the wider university community, puts a specific, named institutional vendor relationship under direct challenge rather than debating AI policy in the abstract.
*For educators:* Would you feel comfortable publicly challenging a signed AI vendor contract at your own institution, and what would have to be in that contract for you to consider it a red line?

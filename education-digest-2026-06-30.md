# AI in Education Digest — 30 June 2026

**UK / Australia / Asia** — Universities are reversing decades of coursework-heavy assessment as a Turnitin survey finds 95% of students now report using AI in their studies (up from 66% in 2024): the University of Bath is piloting a "Two-Lane" system separating open (AI-permitted) and closed (invigilated) assessments, while LSE and Australia's Deakin University are expanding in-person exams. Wearable AI devices—smart glasses and hidden earphones used covertly in Asian exam halls—have emerged as a threat researchers describe as having "no defence," since they are virtually undetectable by human proctors.
*For educators:* If wearable AI makes even in-person invigilated exams increasingly unreliable, which assessment formats—oral defences, real-time problem-solving, portfolio reviews—might better reveal what a student genuinely understands rather than what AI can supply on their behalf?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/depth/are-universities-returning-person-exams-combat-ai-cheating

**UK** — The University of Surrey has undertaken a "systematic redesign of every degree programme" to embed AI in discipline-specific ways—law students examine IP implications of AI-generated content, politics students interrogate whether ChatGPT correctly explains an election result—with a full AI in Education Framework launching across all subjects in September 2026. Assessments institution-wide are being redesigned to prioritise process, reasoning, and reflection over outputs that AI could produce with minimal student effort.
*For educators:* What would it look like to redesign your discipline's signature assessments so that fluency with AI tools specific to your field—not generic "prompt literacy"—becomes an explicit and measurable learning outcome?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/surrey-redesigns-all-degrees-embed-discipline-specific-ai

**EU / Europe** — European universities are discovering that many of their most routine AI uses—automated admissions screening, AI-assisted grading, online exam behaviour monitoring—fall under the EU AI Act's "high-risk" category, with compliance obligations for those systems arriving in August 2026. Even informal faculty practices, such as pasting student work into a free AI chatbot, may breach the Act's transparency and data requirements, meaning institutions must audit not only licensed platforms but individual staff habits.
*For educators:* How would you go about identifying every AI system your department currently relies on—formal or informal—if an audit required you to classify each one as high-risk or not, and document the decision?

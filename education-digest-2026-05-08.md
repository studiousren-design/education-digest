# AI in Education Digest — 2026-05-08

**[UK — Employers & Higher Education]** — The Institute of Student Employers' Student Development Survey 2026, polling 144 organisations, found that 87% of employers expect AI to reshape graduate and apprentice roles, with 54% naming AI's impact on entry-level development as their single biggest concern. Yet most expect only minor adjustments to job tasks, not wholesale transformation, while 35% still rate graduates below expectations for adapting to workplace demands in an AI-reshaping environment.
*For educators:* As employers increasingly name adaptability — rather than any particular technical skill — as their top graduate hiring criterion, how are your programmes actively building and evidencing that capacity rather than simply claiming it?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/employers-fear-graduates-lack-adaptability-ai-reshapes-work

**[UK — Healthcare Higher Education, University of Hull]** — Writing from the University of Hull, Andrew Barker argues that AI is entering clinical healthcare practice faster than most curricula are adapting, and that the central failure is one of framing: institutions are still debating generative AI for student assignments when the real question is how future health professionals will work safely and accountably with AI-enabled systems in regulated care settings. Rather than adding a standalone AI module, Barker proposes reviewing existing taught content to integrate AI literacy — what it does, where it fails, and who is responsible when it goes wrong — into the professional frameworks where it is already most relevant.
*For educators:* If your field is regulated and safety-critical, what would it take to shift your institution's AI conversation from academic integrity policing to preparing graduates for accountable, AI-assisted professional practice?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/what-does-ai-literacy-healthcare-look

**[International — Higher Education]** — A THE Campus piece argues that generative AI has quietly transformed many academics from facilitators of learning into auditors of process: educators now spend significant time verifying AI use, translating broad institutional policies into messy classroom realities, and adjudicating integrity edge cases — tasks that are time-consuming, repetitive, and emotionally draining. The piece warns that this compliance burden is not just a workload problem but a professional identity problem, as teaching loses its meaning when enforcement crowds out facilitation.
*For educators:* When your institution's AI integrity policy adds more to your administrative load than it removes from your cognitive bandwidth as a teacher, who should be responsible for redesigning it — and what would you need to make the case for change?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/genai-has-turned-teachers-auditors

**[UK / Higher Education Policy]** — A learning expert at Google DeepMind has warned that blanket bans on AI in universities risk shutting down essential conversations about pedagogical innovation, arguing instead for policies that specify *when* and *for which tasks* AI use is acceptable. The intervention comes as HEPI's 2026 Student Generative AI Survey reports that 95 per cent of UK undergraduates now use generative AI, with the share who include AI-generated text directly in submissions rising from 3 per cent in 2024 to 12 per cent today.

*For educators:* If the choice is no longer between "ban it" or "allow it" but between thoughtfully defining acceptable use or having no policy at all, who in your institution owns that specification work — and are they resourced to do it?

**[USA / University of Florida]** — The University of Florida's AI2 Centre has spent several years embedding AI literacy across every undergraduate major — through a university-wide Certificate in AI Fundamentals and Applications — making it one of the first US institutions to take a whole-of-curriculum rather than elective approach, begun before ChatGPT arrived. Director Hans van Oostrom reports that the process has exposed uneven faculty adoption and persistent resistance in disciplines where AI's relevance is less immediately obvious, pointing to the need for field-specific rather than generic AI integration support.

*For educators:* When a university commits to AI "across all majors," whose job is it to translate that mandate into meaningful learning outcomes for a student studying music history or nursing — and what happens when those faculty are not convinced?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/campus-talks-what-does-ai-across-curriculum-look-practice

**[UK / Publishing Education]** — In the wake of Hachette's March 2026 decision to withdraw Mia Ballard's novel *Shy Girl* after the author was accused of using generative AI, publishing educators have designed exercises that use AI's own limitations as teaching tools: students attempt real publishing workflows with AI assistance and discover firsthand where the technology falls short of human editorial judgement. The approach inverts the usual framing — rather than warning students away from AI, it tasks them with locating what remains irreducibly human in creative and publishing work.

*For educators:* If students can only fully recognise the value of human creativity by first experiencing AI's inadequacy, does that mean we need to let them fail with AI before they can succeed without it — and what does that imply for how we sequence AI exposure in creative disciplines?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/equip-students-champion-human-creativity-over-machine-outputs

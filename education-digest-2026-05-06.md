# Education Digest — 6 May 2026

---

**[Asia — Multi-country higher education: Hong Kong, Indonesia, Japan, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore, South Korea, Thailand]** — A survey spanning eight Asian higher-education systems, presented at the THE Asia Universities Summit in Hong Kong, found that institutions in leading economies such as Singapore and Hong Kong already deploy AI tutors and chatbots, while most others across the region have strong digital foundations but lack the coordinated investment in infrastructure, pedagogy, research capacity, and leadership needed to scale impact. The research concludes that piecemeal tool adoption is insufficient and that sustained AI benefit requires simultaneous, system-wide investment across all pillars.
*For educators:* If even the region's most digitally mature universities are struggling to move from individual AI tools to institutional transformation, what does genuine "AI readiness" require — and who is responsible for building it?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

**[UK — University sector (University of Surrey / Jisc)]** — Research led by Professor Naomi Winstone at the University of Surrey warns that outsourcing student feedback to AI risks eroding two decades of pedagogical scholarship establishing feedback as a dialogic process rather than a deliverable product — and that academics themselves may progressively lose the professional skill of giving feedback if they rely on AI tools to do it. A national trial coordinated by Jisc is currently evaluating AI feedback platforms in universities under pressure from large class sizes and resource constraints.
*For educators:* If AI feedback tools are introduced primarily to protect teacher time under resource pressure, how do institutions ensure that the human expertise behind high-quality feedback is actively maintained rather than quietly atrophied?
Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/rss

---

**[USA — K–12 and higher education]** — An EdSurge analysis argues that AI's genuine educational value lies not in replacing effort but in eliminating "unproductive struggle" — such as decoding unfamiliar vocabulary or navigating formatting conventions — while preserving the "productive struggle" that drives durable learning. Tools that adjust reading levels in real time for below-grade-level students and English-language learners are offered as a model: AI removes the linguistic barrier so learners can still grapple fully with the intellectual content.
*For educators:* As AI becomes increasingly capable of scaffolding away cognitive friction, how should educators draw the line between obstacles that are worth removing and difficulties that are educationally essential to work through?
Source: https://www.edsurge.com/rss

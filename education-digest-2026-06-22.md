# AI in Education Digest — 22 June 2026

**US / K–12 School Districts** — Unlike traditional software that becomes cheaper to distribute as it scales, generative AI keeps generating costs with every student interaction — what the industry calls "inference costs" — meaning schools face an entirely new economic model as they try to sustain AI-first classrooms. EdSurge reports that districts accustomed to predictable per-seat licensing are struggling to project AI spending, raising urgent questions about whether widespread student-facing AI is financially viable at all.

*For educators:* If every student conversation with an AI tool carries a real-time compute cost, how should schools decide which uses of AI are worth paying for — and who in a district should make that judgement?

Source: https://www.edsurge.com/news/can-schools-afford-an-ai-first-future

---

**UK / Higher Education** — Miriam Schneider, director of learning initiatives at Google DeepMind, argues in Times Higher Education that blanket AI bans in universities are counterproductive, as the confrontation between what remains uniquely human and what AI can do "can have a meaningful role to play" in pedagogy. Her comments coincide with tech companies responding to academic-integrity fears by launching constrained "guided learning" modes — such as Google's Gemini LearnLM — rather than restricting student access to AI tools outright.

*For educators:* When a commercial AI company designs a "learning mode" intended to prevent misuse, what obligation does a university have to independently evaluate whether that company's definition of learning aligns with its own academic values?

Source: https://www.timeshighereducation.com/news/all-or-nothing-approach-ai-risks-shutting-down-innovation

---

**US / K–12** — A new EdSurge analysis warns against "cognitive surrender" — the growing tendency for students to accept AI-generated answers with minimal scrutiny — arguing that human educators provide irreplaceable friction and relational context that AI cannot replicate. The piece draws on recent research to argue that education's core purpose is not information delivery but building students' capacity to assess quality, connect ideas, and engage with complexity.

*For educators:* How would you distinguish, in everyday classroom work, between a student using AI as a genuine thinking scaffold and one who has quietly outsourced the thinking itself?
